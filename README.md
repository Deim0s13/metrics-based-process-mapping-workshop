# Metrics Based Process Mapping Workshop

A practical, vendor-neutral workshop for helping teams identify automation opportunities using process mapping, lightweight metrics, and evidence-based prioritisation.

> **Map the work. Measure the pain. Automate with purpose.**

---

## Overview

This repository contains the source materials for a 90-minute workshop designed to help teams explore automation opportunities in a structured, evidence-led way.

The workshop introduces **Metrics Based Process Mapping** as a lightweight method for understanding how work happens, where effort, delay, rework or risk exists, and whether automation is the right response.

The aim is to help teams move from assumption-led automation ideas such as:

* “This process is painful.”
* “This should be automated.”
* “This takes too long.”
* “We need a workflow, bot or tool.”

To better questions such as:

* What is actually happening?
* Where is the pain occurring?
* What evidence do we have?
* Is automation the right response?
* Is the process valuable, feasible and ready to automate?
* What should we prioritise first?

The workshop is intended to be delivered as a guided conversation, supported by practical templates and scenario-based discussion.

---

## What this is

This is a reusable workshop kit for helping teams identify, assess and prioritise automation opportunities.

It provides:

* a 90-minute workshop structure
* facilitator guidance
* participant pre-work
* slide source content
* scenario clinic prompts
* reusable templates
* example process mapping content
* a simple opportunity assessment approach

The workshop is designed to help teams:

1. Map how work happens today.
2. Add useful metrics to understand the pain.
3. Identify whether the issue is effort, flow, quality, risk or experience.
4. Decide whether automation, process improvement, standardisation or further investigation is the right response.
5. Prioritise opportunities based on value, feasibility and readiness.

---

## What this is not

This is not a tooling workshop.

It does not teach a specific automation platform, workflow tool, RPA product or AI solution.

It is also not:

* a formal Lean Six Sigma course
* a full BPMN modelling exercise
* a detailed process mining implementation guide
* a technical automation design session
* a replacement for detailed discovery, solution architecture or delivery planning
* intended to produce a final automation solution in the room

The workshop is a lightweight discovery method. It helps teams understand the work, evidence the pain, and decide the next best action.

## Who this is for

This workshop is intended for teams that are starting to explore automation opportunities across their organisation or business area.

It may be useful for:

* automation teams
* platform teams
* operations teams
* service delivery teams
* business improvement teams
* transformation teams
* process owners
* business analysts
* product owners
* technology teams working with business stakeholders

The audience does not need to be experienced in process mapping or automation discovery. The workshop is designed to introduce the concepts in plain language and then apply them to real scenarios.

## When to use this workshop

Use this workshop when a team is asking questions such as:

* Where should we start with automation?
* Which manual processes are worth automating?
* How do we avoid automating the wrong thing?
* How do we prioritise automation opportunities?
* How do we compare process pain points fairly?
* How do we build a small, evidence-based automation backlog?

It is especially useful when there are many automation ideas, but limited evidence about which ones are valuable, feasible or ready.

## Core workshop message

The workshop is built around one repeated anchor:

> **Map the work. Measure the pain. Automate with purpose.**

The supporting model is:

```text
Map the work → Measure the pain → Decide the response → Prioritise the opportunity
```

This model helps teams avoid jumping straight to solutions. It encourages them to first understand the process, gather enough evidence, and decide whether automation is genuinely the right next step.

## The narrative

Every part of the workshop supports a simple story:

> Before deciding what to automate, understand what is actually happening.

That story plays out in four parts.

| Section   | Narrative beat                                  | Core question                                       |
| --------- | ----------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Why**   | Automation discovery needs evidence             | Why should we slow down before choosing a solution? |
| **What**  | Metrics Based Process Mapping gives us a method | What does this approach help us see?                |
| **How**   | We map, measure and assess                      | How do we apply this in practice?                   |
| **Apply** | We test the approach against real scenarios     | What is the next best action for this opportunity?  |

The session is designed to feel like a guided conversation rather than a methodology lecture.

## Methods this workshop draws from

Metrics Based Process Mapping is a practical blended approach. It draws from recognised practices including:

| Method / discipline             | What it contributes                                       |
| ------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Process mapping**             | Steps, decisions, roles, systems, handoffs and exceptions |
| **Value stream mapping / Lean** | Flow, wait time, cycle time, waste and value              |
| **Business process management** | Process ownership, standardisation and improvement        |
| **Process measurement**         | Baseline data, performance indicators and evidence        |
| **Automation discovery**        | Value, feasibility, readiness and prioritisation          |

The intent is not to apply a heavy methodology. The intent is to use these practices in a lightweight, practical way to support better automation decisions.

## Workshop format

The workshop is designed as a **90-minute core session**, ideally scheduled inside a **2-hour calendar hold**.

This allows space for:

* late starts
* questions
* deeper discussion
* additional real scenarios
* overrun without rushing the close

### Recommended timing

|       Time | Section           | Focus                                       |
| ---------: | ----------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
|   0–5 mins | Welcome and setup | Purpose, structure and expectations         |
|  5–15 mins | Why               | Why automation discovery needs evidence     |
| 15–30 mins | What              | What Metrics Based Process Mapping gives us |
| 30–60 mins | How               | How to map, measure and assess              |
| 60–85 mins | Apply             | Scenario clinic using real examples         |
| 85–90 mins | Wrap-up           | Next steps and close                        |

The remaining 30 minutes in the calendar hold can be used for additional scenario discussion, Q&A or follow-up planning.

---

## Learning outcomes

By the end of the workshop, participants should be able to:

1. Explain why automation discovery should be evidence-led.
2. Describe the core idea of Metrics Based Process Mapping.
3. Map a process at a practical discovery level.
4. Identify useful metrics such as volume, cycle time, touch time, wait time, handoffs, rework, exceptions and risk.
5. Distinguish between process pain, process improvement and automation opportunity.
6. Assess automation candidates using value, feasibility and readiness.
7. Categorise opportunities as:

   * automate now
   * improve first
   * investigate further
   * do not automate yet
8. Identify the next best action for a candidate automation opportunity.

---

## Expected outputs

This workshop does not aim to produce a complete automation roadmap in one session.

Expected outputs are:

* a shared understanding of the discovery method
* one or more real scenarios tested against the method
* early categorisation of candidate opportunities
* a list of missing evidence or metrics
* agreement on next best actions
* a small set of candidate processes for deeper follow-up

A successful session should help the team decide what to investigate next, not force them to solve the full process in the room.

---

## Opportunity categories

The workshop uses four simple categories to help teams decide what to do next.

| Category                | Meaning                                      | Typical next action                                |
| ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **Automate now**        | High value, feasible and ready               | Move into automation discovery or design           |
| **Improve first**       | Worth solving, but process needs tidying     | Clarify, simplify, standardise or assign ownership |
| **Investigate further** | Potential value, but more evidence is needed | Gather data or assess technical feasibility        |
| **Do not automate yet** | Low value, unstable or unclear               | Defer or address underlying process issues         |

The key point is that “not ready for automation” does not mean “not worth improving”.

## Pre-work guidance

Participants should be asked to bring one candidate process.

A good candidate process might be:

* manual
* repetitive
* slow
* high-volume
* risky
* inconsistent
* approval-heavy
* spreadsheet-heavy
* dependent on email handoffs
* creating rework or errors
* difficult to track
* frustrating for users or customers

Participants do not need to know whether it should be automated. That is what the workshop helps test.

Suggested pre-work prompt:

> Please come prepared with one process that may be a candidate for automation. It does not need to be fully documented. A good example might be something that is manual, repetitive, slow, high-volume, risky, inconsistent or creates rework.

Ask participants to think about:

* What starts the process?
* What outcome is needed?
* Who is involved?
* What systems are used?
* Where does it slow down?
* Where is manual effort involved?
* What tends to go wrong?
* What data or metrics do you already have?

## Facilitation principles

The workshop works best when it is run as a guided conversation.

### Keep it practical

Avoid turning the session into a detailed methodology lesson. The goal is to help the group use the method, not master process theory.

### Start with the work

When the group jumps to a tool or solution, bring them back to the process.

Useful prompts:

* What is the process we are trying to improve?
* Where does it start and end?
* Who is involved?
* Where is the pain?
* What evidence do we have?

### Separate pain from evidence

Pain is a useful signal, but it needs to be tested.

Useful prompts:

* How often does this happen?
* How long does it take?
* How much manual effort is involved?
* How often does it go wrong?
* What is the impact if it fails?

### Avoid jumping straight to automation

Automation is one possible response, not the only response.

Other responses may include:

* clearer ownership
* better intake
* process simplification
* standardisation
* validation
* policy clarification
* workflow visibility
* better reporting
* system integration
* further technical investigation

### Focus on the next best action

The scenario clinic does not need to solve the full process.

For each scenario, aim to agree:

1. likely category
2. missing evidence
3. next best action
4. who should be involved next

## Common facilitation challenges

### The group jumps straight to a tool

Bring the conversation back to the process.

Prompt:

> Before we decide the solution, what is the process telling us?

---

### The group debates too much detail

Keep the discussion at discovery level.

Prompt:

> That detail may be important later, but for now what is the core pain point and what evidence do we need?

---

### The group cannot agree on how the process works

Treat this as useful information.

Prompt:

> That may tell us the process is not as standard as we thought. Let’s capture the different paths rather than forcing agreement too early.

---

### The process is painful but unclear

Categorise it as “improve first” or “investigate further”.

Prompt:

> This may be worth solving, but it may need clearer ownership, rules or evidence before automation is sensible.

---

### The automation idea is technically possible but low value

Acknowledge feasibility, then return to value.

Prompt:

> It sounds possible, but is it valuable enough to prioritise compared with other opportunities?

## Scenario clinic structure

Use the scenario clinic to test actual processes or automation ideas.

For each scenario, ask:

1. What is the process, and where does it start and end?
2. Where is the pain — effort, delay, errors, risk, rework or something else?
3. What evidence do we have, and what are we missing?
4. Is the right response automation, improvement or both?
5. Where does this land: automate now, improve first, investigate further or do not automate yet?
6. What is the next best action?

The scenario clinic should not try to solve everything in the room. Its purpose is to identify whether the opportunity is worth taking forward and what should happen next.

## Metrics used in the workshop

The workshop groups metrics into three practical categories.

### Effort

| Metric            | What it helps show            |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------- |
| **Volume**        | How often the process happens |
| **Touch time**    | Actual human effort involved  |
| **Manual effort** | Repetitive or low-value work  |

### Flow

| Metric           | What it helps show                                   |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cycle time**   | Total elapsed time from start to finish              |
| **Wait time**    | Time spent idle between steps                        |
| **Handoffs**     | Movement between people or teams                     |
| **Systems used** | Tools, forms or platforms touched across the process |

### Quality and risk

| Metric              | What it helps show                              |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **Rework / errors** | How often work needs correction                 |
| **Exceptions**      | Deviations from the standard path               |
| **Risk impact**     | What happens if something goes wrong            |
| **User impact**     | Who experiences the pain, delay or poor outcome |

You do not need every metric for every process. Choose the metrics that best explain the pain and value.

## Assessment lenses

The workshop uses three lenses to assess automation opportunities.

| Lens            | Key question           | What to look for                                                 |
| --------------- | ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Value**       | Is this worth solving? | Time saved, risk reduced, rework reduced, better experience      |
| **Feasibility** | Can we automate it?    | Clear rules, stable data, system access, technical pathway       |
| **Readiness**   | Is the process ready?  | Process owner, agreed steps, stable process, stakeholder support |

A strong automation candidate is not just technically possible. It is valuable, feasible and ready.

---

## Strong and less ready automation candidates

### Strong candidates usually have

* regular or high volume
* repetitive steps
* clear rules and decision logic
* stable inputs and outputs
* significant manual effort
* measurable errors or rework
* accessible systems or data
* clear process owner

### Less ready candidates often have

* low volume
* high judgement required
* unclear decision-making
* poor data quality
* too many exceptions
* unstable or evolving process
* unresolved policy or governance questions
* unclear ownership

Not ready for automation does not mean not worth improving.

## Example workshop close

A useful way to close the session:

> The main takeaway is that automation discovery should be evidence-based. We are not just asking what is annoying or manual. We are asking where the process creates effort, delay, risk, rework or poor experience — and whether automation is the right response.
>
> The next step is to take a small number of candidate processes, map them properly, gather the missing metrics, and decide whether they are ready to automate, need improvement first, need further investigation or should be deferred.
>
> Map the work. Measure the pain. Automate with purpose.

## Adapting the workshop

This workshop is intentionally lightweight and can be adapted.

Possible adaptations include:

* reducing it to a 60-minute introduction
* expanding the scenario clinic into a separate follow-up session
* using it as part of a broader automation discovery programme
* adding organisation-specific automation principles
* adding a specific tooling assessment after the process discovery step
* converting the templates into Miro, Mural, FigJam, Excel or Google Sheets formats

If adapting the workshop, try to preserve the core sequence:

```text
Map the work → Measure the pain → Decide the response → Prioritise the opportunity
```

## Suggested follow-up session

A follow-up session may be useful if the team needs help applying the method independently.

A simple follow-up could be 45–60 minutes and focus on reviewing 2–3 mapped candidate processes.

Suggested follow-up agenda:

|       Time | Focus                                   |
| ---------: | --------------------------------------- |
|   0–5 mins | Recap the method                        |
|  5–35 mins | Review candidate processes              |
| 35–50 mins | Assess value, feasibility and readiness |
| 50–60 mins | Confirm categories and next actions     |

The follow-up session should help the team test whether their mapped scenarios are clear enough to move into automation discovery, improvement or further investigation.
