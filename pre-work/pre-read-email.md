# Pre-read: Metrics Based Process Mapping Workshop

This short pre-read is designed to help you prepare for the Metrics Based Process Mapping workshop.

You do not need to complete a detailed process map before the session. The main ask is that you come prepared with one real process, workflow or activity that may be a candidate for automation.

---

## Workshop purpose

The workshop will introduce a practical way to identify automation opportunities using process mapping and lightweight metrics.

The aim is to help us move from statements like:

* “This process is painful.”
* “This should be automated.”
* “This takes too long.”
* “We need a workflow, bot or tool.”

To better questions like:

* What is actually happening?
* Where is the pain occurring?
* What evidence do we have?
* Is automation the right response?
* Is the process ready to automate?
* What should we do next?

The goal is not to automate everything. The goal is to identify the right opportunities and make better decisions about what to automate, improve, investigate or defer.

---

## Core idea

The workshop is built around one simple idea:

> **Map the work. Measure the pain. Automate with purpose.**

This means:

| Step                           | What it means                                                                         |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Map the work**               | Understand how the process happens today                                              |
| **Measure the pain**           | Add evidence about effort, delay, rework, risk or impact                              |
| **Decide the response**        | Determine whether automation, improvement, standardisation or investigation is needed |
| **Prioritise the opportunity** | Focus on the candidates that are valuable, feasible and ready                         |

---

## What to bring

Please come prepared with **one process, workflow or activity** that may be a candidate for automation.

It does not need to be fully documented. A few notes are enough.

A good example might be something that is:

* manual
* repetitive
* slow
* high-volume
* risky
* inconsistent
* difficult to track
* approval-heavy
* spreadsheet-heavy
* dependent on email handoffs
* creating rework, duplication or frustration

You do not need to know whether it should be automated. We will work through that during the session.

---

## What makes a good candidate process?

For the workshop, the best examples are usually processes where people can describe what happens today and where there is a clear pain point.

Good workshop examples often include:

| Example type                          | Why it can be useful                                                   |
| ------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Manual request processes**          | Often involve handoffs, approvals, rework and status chasing           |
| **Reporting processes**               | Often involve repeated data gathering, copying, formatting or checking |
| **Access or onboarding processes**    | Often involve multiple systems, approvals and repeated steps           |
| **Compliance or evidence collection** | Often involves risk, traceability, rework or manual checking           |
| **Service desk or triage processes**  | Often involves volume, routing, categorisation and repeated actions    |
| **Approval workflows**                | Often involves wait time, unclear ownership or escalation issues       |
| **Spreadsheet-based tracking**        | Often involves duplication, manual updates and visibility problems     |

Avoid bringing a process that is too broad, such as “all onboarding” or “all reporting”. Try to choose a specific process or workflow within that broader area.

For example:

| Too broad   | Better                                          |
| ----------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Onboarding  | Requesting system access for a new starter      |
| Reporting   | Creating the weekly operational status report   |
| Finance     | Approving low-value supplier invoices           |
| IT requests | Triage of standard service desk access requests |
| Compliance  | Collecting evidence for a monthly control check |

## Questions to think about before the workshop

Use the questions below to prepare your example. You do not need perfect answers.

### 1. What is the process?

Give it a simple name.

Examples:

* New starter access request
* Weekly reporting process
* Supplier invoice approval
* Compliance evidence collection
* Service request triage

### 2. What starts the process?

Think about the trigger.

Examples:

* A form is submitted
* An email is received
* A request is logged
* A report is due
* A customer asks for something
* A scheduled review is required

### 3. What outcome is needed?

Think about what “done” looks like.

Examples:

* Access has been provisioned
* A report has been sent
* An approval has been completed
* A record has been updated
* Evidence has been collected
* A request has been resolved

### 4. Who is involved?

Think about the people, roles or teams that touch the process.

Examples:

* requester
* approver
* operations team
* service desk
* finance team
* compliance team
* technology team
* customer or end user

### 5. What systems, tools or documents are used?

Think about all the places work happens.

Examples:

* email
* forms
* spreadsheets
* service desk tools
* workflow systems
* finance systems
* identity systems
* CRM or ERP systems
* shared drives
* reporting tools

### 6. Where does the process slow down?

Think about delays, bottlenecks and waiting.

Examples:

* waiting for approval
* waiting for missing information
* waiting for another team
* waiting for someone to check a spreadsheet
* waiting for a manual update
* waiting for a response to an email

### 7. Where is manual effort involved?

Think about repeated human work.

Examples:

* copying data between systems
* checking information manually
* sending reminder emails
* updating spreadsheets
* creating reports
* reconciling information
* manually assigning or routing work

### 8. What tends to go wrong?

Think about errors, rework or exceptions.

Examples:

* incomplete information
* wrong data entered
* approvals missed
* duplicate requests
* unclear ownership
* inconsistent decisions
* work lost in email
* too many exceptions

### 9. What evidence or metrics do you already have?

Think about any rough data or estimates.

Examples:

* how often the process happens
* how long it takes end to end
* how much time people spend on it
* how often it gets stuck
* how often rework is needed
* how often information is missing
* how many people or teams are involved
* what happens if something goes wrong

Estimates are fine. The important thing is to be clear about what is known and what still needs to be validated.

## Useful metrics to think about

You do not need to collect all of these before the workshop. They are provided to help you think about the type of evidence that may be useful.

### Effort

| Metric            | Question                                             |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Volume**        | How often does this happen?                          |
| **Touch time**    | How much actual human effort is involved?            |
| **Manual effort** | Where are people doing repetitive or low-value work? |

### Flow

| Metric           | Question                                                       |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cycle time**   | How long does the process take from start to finish?           |
| **Wait time**    | Where does work sit idle between steps?                        |
| **Handoffs**     | Where does work move between people or teams?                  |
| **Systems used** | What tools, forms or platforms are touched across the process? |

### Quality and risk

| Metric              | Question                                                   |
| ------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Rework / errors** | How often does work need to be corrected?                  |
| **Exceptions**      | How often does the process deviate from the standard path? |
| **Risk impact**     | What happens if something goes wrong?                      |
| **User impact**     | Who experiences the pain, delay or poor outcome?           |

You do not need every metric. Choose the ones that help explain the pain and value.

## Important distinction: time is not one thing

When looking at automation opportunities, it is useful to separate three types of time.

| Type of time   | Meaning                                 | Example                                 |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Cycle time** | Total elapsed time from start to finish | A request takes five days end to end    |
| **Touch time** | Actual human effort                     | Someone spends 25 minutes processing it |
| **Wait time**  | Time sitting idle between steps         | It waits three days for approval        |

A process may feel slow, but the actual human effort may be small.

For example, a process may take five days from start to finish, but only involve 25 minutes of actual work. The rest may be waiting for approvals, information or handoffs.

That matters because the right response may not be full automation. It may be:

* better routing
* clearer approvals
* automated reminders
* better intake
* fewer handoffs
* improved visibility

---

## Automation is one possible response

The workshop will help test whether automation is the right response, but it is not the only possible response.

| Pain point              | Possible response                                 |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Missing information     | Better form, validation or guidance               |
| Manual data entry       | Integration or data transfer automation           |
| Long approval delay     | Workflow routing, reminders or escalation         |
| Repeated status chasing | Automated notifications or dashboard              |
| Frequent rework         | Standardisation, validation or clearer rules      |
| Inconsistent decisions  | Decision tree, policy clarification or guardrails |
| Too many exceptions     | Process redesign before automation                |
| Poor visibility         | Tracking, reporting or workflow transparency      |

Sometimes the best first step is to simplify, standardise or clarify the process before automation.

---

## How opportunities will be assessed

During the workshop, we will assess candidate opportunities using three lenses.

| Lens            | Key question           | What we look for                                                 |
| --------------- | ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Value**       | Is this worth solving? | Time saved, risk reduced, rework reduced, better experience      |
| **Feasibility** | Can we automate it?    | Clear rules, stable data, system access, technical pathway       |
| **Readiness**   | Is the process ready?  | Process owner, agreed steps, stable process, stakeholder support |

A strong automation candidate is not just technically possible. It should be valuable, feasible and ready.

---

## Possible outcomes from the workshop

Your example may land in one of four categories.

| Category                | Meaning                                      | Typical next action                                |
| ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **Automate now**        | High value, feasible and ready               | Move into automation discovery or design           |
| **Improve first**       | Worth solving, but process needs tidying     | Clarify, simplify, standardise or assign ownership |
| **Investigate further** | Potential value, but more evidence is needed | Gather data or assess technical feasibility        |
| **Do not automate yet** | Low value, unstable or unclear               | Defer or address underlying process issues         |

Not ready for automation does not mean not worth improving.

---

## How to prepare in 10 minutes

If you only have a short time to prepare, answer these five questions:

1. What process or workflow do you want to discuss?
2. What makes it painful, slow, risky or frustrating?
3. Who is involved and what systems are used?
4. What evidence do you already have, even if it is only an estimate?
5. What would improve if this process worked better?

That is enough to participate in the workshop.

---

## What you do not need to do

You do not need to:

* create a full process map
* prepare a slide deck
* collect perfect data
* know the automation solution
* prove the business case in advance
* have all stakeholders aligned before the session

The workshop is designed to help structure the first stage of discovery.
